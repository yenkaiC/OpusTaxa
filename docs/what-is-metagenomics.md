# What is Metagenomics?

Metagenomics is the sequencing of genetic material taken directly from an environmental or biological sample without first isolating or culturing individual organisms. Rather than studying one microbe at a time, metagenomics lets you sequence everything present in a sample simultaneously: bacteria, archaea, fungi, viruses, and more.

The result is a snapshot of an entire microbial community, who is there, how abundant they are, and what they are capable of doing.

## Amplicon sequencing (e.g. 16S rRNA)

The most widely used approach in microbiome research has historically been **amplicon sequencing**, most commonly targeting the **16S ribosomal RNA gene** in bacteria and archaea.

The 16S rRNA gene contains regions that are highly conserved across all bacteria meaning a single pair of PCR primers can amplify a fragment from almost any bacterium in a sample. The amplified fragments are then sequenced and compared to reference databases to identify which organisms are present.

**What 16S tells you:**
- Which bacterial and archaeal taxa are present
- Their relative proportions in the community

**Limitations of 16S:**
- It targets only one gene, so resolution at the species level is often poor — many organisms share near-identical 16S sequences
- It cannot detect fungi, viruses, or other non-bacterial microbes
- It provides no information about what the community is *doing* no functional or metabolic data
- Results can be influenced by which region of the 16S gene is targeted and which primers are used, making cross-study comparisons tricky

16S is cost-effective and well-established, and remains a reasonable choice when community composition at a coarse taxonomic level is sufficient. But it is not what OpusTaxa is designed for.

## Shotgun metagenomics

**Shotgun metagenomics** takes a different approach. Instead of amplifying a single target gene, the entire DNA content of the sample is fragmented and sequenced. Every organism in the sample contributes sequence reads in proportion to its abundance bacteria, archaea, fungi, viruses, and even host DNA.

**What shotgun metagenomics tells you:**
- Which organisms are present, identified with much higher species-level resolution than 16S
- Their relative abundances
- What metabolic pathways and functions are encoded in the community
- Antibiotic resistance genes, biosynthetic gene clusters, and other functional elements
- Viral community composition
- Strain-level variation within species (with sufficient depth)

The trade-off is cost and complexity. Shotgun sequencing generates far more data than 16S, requires more computational resources to analyse, and needs careful host read removal when working with samples like stool, tissue, or saliva that contain substantial human DNA.

**OpusTaxa is designed for shotgun metagenomics.** Specifically, it processes short paired-end reads generated by platforms such as Illumina or MGI — typically 150 bp reads sequenced from both ends of a DNA fragment.

## Long-read metagenomics

A third approach uses **long-read sequencing** platforms such as Oxford Nanopore or PacBio, which generate reads that are thousands to tens of thousands of base pairs long rather than 150 bp.

Longer reads offer advantages for metagenomics: they span repetitive regions that short reads cannot resolve, they can improve genome assembly quality, and they can sometimes distinguish closely related strains more easily. However, long reads currently have higher error rates than short Illumina reads (though this is improving rapidly), and the higher cost per base means sequencing depth is typically lower.

**OpusTaxa does not currently support long-read data.** The tools it uses MetaPhlAn, SingleM, Kraken2, HUMAnN, and others are optimised for short paired-end reads. If you have long-read metagenomic data, other specialised workflows would be more appropriate.

## Summary

| | 16S Amplicon | Short-read Shotgun | Long-read Shotgun |
|--|---|---|--|
| **What is sequenced** | One marker gene (PCR-amplified) | Whole community DNA | Whole community DNA |
| **Read length** | ~250–500 bp | ~150 bp (paired) | 1,000–100,000+ bp |
| **Taxonomic resolution** | Genus level (usually) | Species / strain level | Species / strain level |
| **Functional information** | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Virus detection** | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Fungal detection** | Limited (ITS target needed) | Yes | Yes |
| **Cost** | Lower | Moderate | Higher |
| **Supported by OpusTaxa** | No | **Yes** | No |
